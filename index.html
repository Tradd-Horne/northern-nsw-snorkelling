Searching for Fish

An interactive exploration of the marine life beneath the surface
along Northern NSW & Southeast Queensland

April 2026 · 12 min read

Concept, data & development by Tradd Horne

It started with a turtle.

I was floating face-down at Cook Island off Tweed Heads, the water clear enough to see twenty metres, when a green sea turtle the size of a coffee table glided directly beneath me. It looked up — or at least I convinced myself it did — and for a few seconds the entire ocean narrowed to just the two of us.

I went home and Googled everything: green sea turtle size, green sea turtle diet, can green turtles recognise humans. That single encounter pulled me into a world I hadn't known existed right off our coastline.

This project explores that curiosity — what people search for, what they find, and the remarkable underwater world that sits just a mask and snorkel away from Byron Bay to Moreton Bay.

Part One

"I'm Not a Fish Person"

You might not think of yourself as someone who knows anything about marine life. But you know more than you realise. If I asked you to picture a shark, a ray, a clownfish — you'd have an image instantly. We carry a mental catalogue of ocean creatures shaped by documentaries, childhood trips to the aquarium, and yes, Finding Nemo.

When people search online, they tend to start with these broad groups — "shark", "ray", "turtle" — rather than specific species. The chart below shows which groups of marine life generate the most search interest from snorkellers in our region.

Part Two

The Spark Fish

In birdwatching, they call it a "spark bird" — the one encounter that ignites a lifelong fascination. Snorkelling has its own version. For many people along our coastline, it's a particular moment: the first time a leopard shark rests beneath you at Julian Rocks, or a manta ray eclipses the sun overhead at Point Lookout.

From that moment, the generic search — "shark" — becomes specific: "leopard shark Byron Bay", "manta ray season Stradbroke". You start learning names, seasons, behaviours.

Of the hundreds of species found in our waters, only about 20 have significant search interest. These are the spark fish — the ones that make people care enough to learn their names.

Part Three

What We See — and What We Don't

There's a fascinating disconnect between what we search for and what actually lives beneath the surface. Sharks and turtles dominate our curiosity, but the fish you'll encounter most often while snorkelling are the ones you probably can't name — damselfishes guarding their patch of reef, wrasses darting between corals, surgeonfish grazing in schools.

The chart below compares how much we search for each group versus how abundant they actually are on our reefs. The mismatch reveals our bias toward the spectacular over the everyday.

Wrasses and damselfishes — the backbone of any healthy reef — barely register in search data. Meanwhile, species that need urgent conservation, like the grey nurse shark (critically endangered in Australia) and the green sea turtle (endangered), receive enormous attention. In this case, our fascination might actually be helping — public interest drives funding, protection, and awareness.

Part Four

Where to Look

Our stretch of coastline — from Moreton Bay in the north to the Solitary Islands in the south — sits at a remarkable intersection. The warm East Australian Current pushes tropical species south while cool temperate waters hold their ground. The result is one of the most biodiverse marine corridors in the world.

Each snorkelling region has its own character, its own signature species, and its own best season. The map below shows the top sites and what you're most likely to find at each.

Part Five

The Rhythm of the Reef

The ocean here has a pulse. Summer brings warm water, leopard sharks, and manta rays. Winter delivers crystal visibility and grey nurse shark aggregations. Spring migrations paint the reef with tropical visitors, and autumn offers the sweet spot — warm enough for comfort, clear enough to see.

The chart below tracks how snorkelling interest rises and falls through the year, alongside water temperature and visibility — the three factors that shape every snorkelling season.

The peak in February aligns with school holidays, warm water, and the best chance of encountering tropical visitors. But seasoned snorkellers know: winter is underrated. The water drops to 19°C, but visibility can exceed 20 metres. Grey nurse sharks gather at Fish Rock Cave, and the crowds disappear entirely.

Part Six

Paying Attention

You don't need to be a marine biologist. You don't need expensive gear or a boat. All you need is a mask, a snorkel, and the willingness to put your face in the water and look.

This coastline — our coastline — is home to leopard sharks that rest like sleeping dogs on the sand, manta rays with wingspans wider than your car, clownfish that will stare you down from their anemone fortress, and blue gropers so curious they'll follow you around like underwater labradors.

Every species on this page can be seen without scuba certification, without a boat, and often without even getting your hair wet in anything deeper than chest-height water.

The easiest way to discover this world is simply to pay attention.

About this project

Searching for Fish is a visual exploration of snorkelling and marine life along the Northern NSW and Southeast Queensland coastline. Inspired by Searching for Birds by Nadieh Bremer.

Species data compiled from Atlas of Living Australia, Reef Life Survey, and local dive operator sighting records. Search interest patterns are illustrative.

Data & methodology

This project catalogues marine species commonly encountered while snorkelling at major sites between Moreton Bay (QLD) and the Solitary Islands (NSW). Fish groups, abundance ratings, and seasonal patterns are based on published survey data and local knowledge.

Built with D3.js, vanilla JavaScript, and hand-crafted SVG.
