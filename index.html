Life of Sea

Hi, my name's Yolanda and I'm eight years old.

I love snorkelling, and I think more people should know how amazing it is right here on our coastline. This website is my way of sharing all the good stuff — the best snorkelling spots where you can actually see heaps of fish, what fish you're likely to spot, and what cool creatures to get excited about before you even get in the water.

I also want to show people where different fish and turtles and sea animals like to live, because they don't just swim anywhere — they have favourite habitats, like rocky reefs or seagrass or sandy patches. And did you know some fish and sea creatures live for a really, really long time? I want to share that too, because I think it makes you appreciate them even more when you know how old they might be.

I hope this helps you find somewhere amazing to snorkel — and maybe fall in love with what's under the water, just like I did.

Some fish are everywhere on the reef — like wrasses and damselfishes. You'll see heaps of them! But other creatures, like sharks and sea turtles, are more special because there aren't as many of them.

Wrasses and damselfishes are the most common fish on the reef — they're literally everywhere! But some species need our help. The grey nurse shark is critically endangered in Australia, and green sea turtles are endangered too. That's why it's so important to look after our oceans.

Part Three

Where to Look

Our stretch of coastline — from Moreton Bay in the north to the Solitary Islands in the south — sits at a remarkable intersection. The warm East Australian Current pushes tropical species south while cool temperate waters hold their ground. The result is one of the most biodiverse marine corridors in the world.

Part Four

Best Time to Go

Different times of year are better for seeing different creatures! Summer is warm and that's when leopard sharks and manta rays visit. Winter is colder but the water is super clear — you can see SO far! That's also when grey nurse sharks hang out at Fish Rock Cave.

Summer is really popular for snorkelling because the water is warm and it's school holidays. But here's a secret: winter is actually amazing! The water is only 19°C (you need a wetsuit!) but you can see over 20 metres. Plus there are way less people around.

Part Five

Go Explore!

You don't need to be a marine biologist. You don't need expensive gear or a boat. All you need is a mask, a snorkel, and the willingness to put your face in the water and look.

This coastline — our coastline — is home to leopard sharks that rest like sleeping dogs on the sand, manta rays with wingspans wider than your car, clownfish that will stare you down from their anemone fortress, and blue gropers so curious they'll follow you around like underwater labradors.

Every species on this page can be seen without scuba certification, without a boat, and often without even getting your hair wet in anything deeper than chest-height water.

The easiest way to discover this world is simply to pay attention.

About this project

Life of Sea is a visual exploration of snorkelling and marine life along the Northern NSW and Southeast Queensland coastline. Inspired by Searching for Birds by Nadieh Bremer.

Species data compiled from Atlas of Living Australia, Reef Life Survey, and local dive operator sighting records.

Data & methodology

This project catalogues marine species commonly encountered while snorkelling at major sites between Moreton Bay (QLD) and the Solitary Islands (NSW). Abundance ratings and seasonal patterns are based on published survey data and local knowledge.
